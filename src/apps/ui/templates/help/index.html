1. What RICD is

RICD tracks Queensland First Nations capital housing delivery end to end — from the funding agreement with a council, through approvals and payments, to construction progress and acquittal.

It replaces spreadsheets with a single source of truth that links the money to the bricks: every dollar committed is traceable to a council, a program, a project and the individual works (dwellings, lots, infrastructure) it pays for. The system covers four broad jobs:

  • Plan & fund — record funding agreements, financial approvals, schedules and payment rules.
  • Deliver — capture projects, their works/addresses, and construction progress.
  • Forecast & pay — project cashflow per program and financial year, and release payments against milestones.
  • Report & govern — monthly trackers, quarterly and stage reports, variations, approvals, and a full audit trail.

2. Roles & permissions

Access is controlled by your role. There are five:

RoleWhoCan do
OfficerFNC frontline staff Full create/edit/delete on all records, across all councils.
ManagerFNC senior staff Same access as Officer (trust-based), plus runtime config (site settings, maintenance) and the approver of record for reports/payments.
Council UserCouncil staff View and work with their own council's records only; submit reports and expense claims. No editing of funding internals.
Council ManagerSenior council staff Same scope as Council User (own council only).
Read OnlyInternal audit / finance / executives Read everything across all councils; cannot change anything.
Council scoping: Council User and Council Manager only ever see records belonging to their own council — projects, schedules, payments and reports are filtered automatically. Trying to open another council's record returns "permission denied".

Who can write? Only Officer and Manager can create/edit/delete the core funding and project records. Council roles are limited to submitting their own reports/claims; Read-Only roles can write nothing.

3. What councils can & can't see

Some financial internals are deliberately hidden from councils:

  • Brief Financial Approvals (BFA) are internal-only. Councils never see the BFA itself — including the contingency hold-back, delegate levels, and cost-centre details.
  • Councils learn only whether funding was approved for their projects/funding schedules, via their own project and schedule pages — never the underlying approval breakdown.
  • The contingency amount FNC holds against a project is masked in council-facing cashflow views; it is released only if needed and is visible to the FNC team alone.
If you are a council user and a figure looks "missing" compared with what FNC quotes, that is by design — the approved funding you see excludes FNC's internal contingency.

4. Features by area

The left-hand menu groups the system into five areas.

Portfolio

  • Dashboard — portfolio totals, project status (on-track / late / overdue), budget, filters by council/program/status.
  • Projects — each project's overview, its child addresses & works (dwellings, lots, infrastructure) with costs, funding, payments, reports and documents in tabs. The project list hides completed projects by default — tick Include completed projects under the State filter to show them (useful for analytics and past cashflow without cluttering active work).
  • Cashflow — forecast of committed vs released spend per program per financial year (see §6).
  • Analytics — aggregate outputs: how many dwellings by type and bedroom count, residential lots, and their costs, totalled by category, council and program.

Funding

  • Funding Agreements — the legal umbrella with a council.
  • Funding Schedules — the per-project funding instrument with a lifecycle (draft → ready → executed → active → superseded) and key dates that cascade to the project.
  • Payment Rules — immutable, versioned rules defining how a schedule's payments split (e.g. 30/60/10).
  • BFA FNC only — Brief Financial Approvals: the pre-condition that releases funding, including per-program splits and contingency. The Add Projects button opens a picker showing only projects with a funding shortfall (total works cost exceeds approved BFA funding). A live warning appears if the sum of all row totals exceeds the selected delegate's approval limit.
  • Funding Notices & Expense Claims — the capped-payment pathway; councils can submit claims against a notice.

Reports

  • Monthly Trackers — per-work construction progress grid; entering dates here drives forecasting and payment timing. The Excel export uses a pivoted layout: one sheet per Work Step Group, rows are addresses/works (showing street address and "N bed Type" on two lines), and columns are the tracker steps. Green = completed (actual date recorded), yellow = fillable, grey = not applicable. Import the filled sheet back to bulk-update progress dates.
  • Quarterly Reports — FS → project → works hierarchy with expenditure and declarations, submitted to the State.
  • Stage Reports — Stage 1 / Stage 2 contract checklists that gate the next payment.

Governance

  • Variations — deeds that change schedules/funding, with their own approval and execution states.
  • Approvals — delegation-based sign-off (amount thresholds determine who must approve a payment).
  • Notices — in-app notifications/announcements.

Administration FNC / staff

  • Councils, Programs, Work Types — reference data.
  • Maintenance — lookup tables, notional costs, work step definitions & groups, stage item groups, Payment Milestone Schedules (see §6), electorates/regions, construction methods, and site settings (e.g. the reports email).
  • Audit Log — every change to a financial record is logged automatically (who, what, when, before/after).

5. The funding lifecycle

The money flows through a defined chain, each step gating the next:

  1. Funding Agreement signed with a council (the legal umbrella).
  2. Brief Financial Approval (BFA) approved internally — sets the approved amount and contingency, split per program.
  3. Funding Schedule created for the project (requires an approved BFA) and executed.
  4. Payments raised against the schedule's payment rule (e.g. 30/60/10). The first approved payment moves the schedule to Active.
  5. Reports (Stage 1 / Stage 2) approved unlock the next payment.
  6. Release & reconcile — released payments are snapshotted against program allocations for traceability, then marked RECONCILED once acquitted.

Use Dashboard → Traceability to "follow the money" from a council down through agreements, schedules, allocations and payments.

6. Cashflow & payment timing

The Cashflow page compares, for each program and financial year: the budgeted amount, the forecast committed (payments with a forecast release date), and the released (actual) amount. Over-committed cells are flagged; payments without a forecast date appear in an Undated Payments panel so you can schedule them.

How a payment's forecast date is set

Every payment is either:

  • Manual — you type the forecast release date yourself, or
  • Scheduled — the date is derived automatically from a Payment Milestone Schedule.

A Payment Milestone Schedule (Maintenance → Payment Milestone Schedules) is attached to a Work Step Group and holds one rule per payment. Each rule anchors a payment to a milestone plus an offset in days:

PaymentAnchored toOffset
First (30%)Project start date+14 days
Second (60%)"Site establishment" work step+14 days
Third (10%)"Final internal clean for handover" work step+14 days

This Standard 30/60/10 schedule is the default and covers most projects. A rule can anchor to the project start, a named work step, or forecast practical completion.

Outliers: need different timing for some works? Clone the Work Step Group (its payment schedule is copied too), rename it, adjust the rules, and apply it to those works — or move the works to a separate project. No code change, and the payment dates follow.

When you edit a schedule or rule, existing scheduled payments are re-derived immediately.

Reconciliation & the notice pathway

  • A payment moves PENDING → RECOMMENDED → APPROVED → RELEASED → RECONCILED. Use the Reconcile button on a released payment to mark it acquitted (the reconciled date is stamped automatically).
  • A payment can optionally name the specific work (dwelling/lot) it funds, so finance can trace a payment down to the physical output.
  • The cashflow page also shows a Notice / Expense-Claim pathway panel. That capped, claim-based stream is funded via Funding Notices + Expense Claims, not Payment rows, so it sits beside the matrix rather than inside it.

7. Monthly tracker automation

The whole point of milestone scheduling: staff update progress in one place.

  • You record a work step's actual/forecast completion in the Monthly Tracker.
  • That updates the canonical work step, which re-runs the project's construction forecast.
  • Any scheduled payment anchored to an affected milestone has its forecast release date rolled automatically — so if site establishment slips 30 days, the second payment's forecast moves 30 days too.
You no longer maintain the Monthly Tracker and separately edit payment dates for cashflow — update the tracker only, and the forecast follows.

8. The data model

The main entities and how they relate:

Organisation & reference

  • Council — a local government / housing provider.
  • Program — a funding program (with per-FY budgets).
  • Work Type — house, duplex, lot, infrastructure… (with notional costs).
  • Work Step Definition / Group — the named construction steps and reusable templates.

Delivery

  • Project — belongs to a council + program; has a state lifecycle.
  • Address / Work — the physical things funded (dwellings, lots), each with a work type, quantity, cost and steps.
  • Work Step — a step instance on a work, with forecast/actual dates (the Monthly Tracker columns).

Funding & money

  • Funding Agreement → Brief Financial Approval (+ items, contingency) → Funding Schedule.
  • Payment Rule — versioned split definition used by a schedule.
  • Payment — raised on a schedule; Manual or Scheduled forecast; allocated per program on release.
  • Payment Milestone Schedule / Rule — when scheduled payments are forecast to release.
  • Funding Notice / Expense Claim — capped claim pathway.

Governance

  • Variation (+ items) — changes to schedules/funding.
  • Approval — delegation-based sign-off.
  • Report — Monthly Tracker, Quarterly, Stage reports.
  • Workflow Action / Audit Log — immutable record of every state change.

9. Limitations & things to know

  • Payments link to a project + funding schedule, and optionally to one specific work. Use the "Funds work" field to trace a payment to a dwelling/lot. Scheduled timing still resolves the milestone across all the project's works (latest date wins for "site establishment" / completion), so keep a project's works on one work-step group for predictable timing; use a cloned group or a separate project for outliers.
  • Scheduled payment dates need their milestone to exist. If a project's works have no "Site establishment" step yet, that payment stays undated until the step (or its date) is recorded.
  • Manual always wins. Setting a payment to Manual, or typing a date, opts it out of automatic rolling.
  • Released payments are locked. Their program allocation is snapshotted at release and never retro-adjusted by later BFA changes.
  • Cost fields on a work are per output (per unit), not totals. There are three tiers in priority order: Estimated cost per output (base, from notional rates or manual entry) → Forecast final cost per output (overrides estimated once entered) → Actual cost per output (authoritative once "Costs finalised" is ticked). The system multiplies whichever tier applies by the work's quantity to get the total. Entering a total instead of a per-unit cost will over-report by a factor of quantity.
  • Audit logging is automatic for financial records and cannot be edited.
